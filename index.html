General Prospecting

Use this script when cold-calling homeowners in a target neighborhood to surface listings or referrals. The goal isn't a sale — it's a conversation.

Pick Your Opener · Swipe to Choose

Pattern interrupts and neighbor-prospecting openers. The first 10 are pattern interrupts — your strongest disarming tools. The last 5 are property-specific and referral-driven. Pick what fits the moment.

Opener The Hang-Up Question
1 / 15
1The Hang-Up Question
The cleanest pattern interrupt — said before they can
"Hi [Name]? This is Jonathan — if I told you I was a Realtor, would you hang up, or give me 30 seconds to tell you why I called?"
Use WhenDefault opener. Disarming because you said it first. Works on almost anyone.
2The Upfront Disarmer
When you want to be maximally transparent
"Hi [Name]? [Name], this is Jonathan — look, I'll be upfront, I'm a Realtor and this is a cold call. Do you want to hang up or will you give me 30 seconds and then decide?"
Use WhenYou want zero pretense. The transparency itself disarms.
3The Polite Acknowledger
When you want a slightly softer landing
"Hi [Name]? [Name], this is Jonathan — I'm a Realtor. I know you probably weren't expecting my call. Do you want to hang up — or can I take 30 seconds to tell you why I called?"
Use WhenThe voice on the other end sounds older or more formal.
4The Vulnerability Play
Real vulnerability — people respond to it
"Hi [Name]? [Name], this is Jonathan — I'm a Realtor, and I didn't know if I should even call you, but would you hate me if I asked you something real quick?"
Use WhenYou want maximum humanization. The "didn't know if I should even call" is a real disarmer.
5The Before You Hang Up Frame
Acknowledge the impulse, then ask
"Hi [Name]? [Name], this is Jonathan — I'm a Realtor, and listen, before you hang up, I was hoping to ask you something really quick. Would that be ok?"
Use WhenYou sense they're already irritated or about to hang up.
6The You're Going to Hate Me
Self-deprecating, light, lands well
"Hi [Name]? [Name], this is Jonathan — you're going to hate me, I am a Realtor. Do you want to hang up or will you give me 30 seconds to tell you why I called?"
Use WhenYour energy is high and you can pull off humor.
7The Wrong Number Bait
Classic interrupt — they want to confirm their name
"I don't know if I have the right number — is this [Name]?"
Use WhenCold call with no specific context. Once they confirm, you have their attention.
8The I'll Be Quick Frame
Hand them control immediately
"Hi [Name]? Jonathan with Keller Williams here — I promise to be quick. Got 30 seconds for one real estate question? After that you decide."
Use WhenYou sound confident and want to project respect for their time.
9The Just-to-See Cross Off
"Cross you off" is uniquely disarming
"Hi [Name]? Jonathan, I'm a Realtor in the area. I'm just going down a short list of homeowners I think might be quietly thinking about a move at some point this year. Did I call the right person, or do I cross you off?"
Use WhenYou want a low-pressure permission ask. They often respond with "well no, maybe..."
10The Don't Hate Me
Direct address, light tone
"[Name], this is Jonathan. Look, don't hate me — I'm a Realtor, and before you hang up, I was hoping to ask you something really quick. Would that be ok?"
Use WhenYou skipped the "is this [Name]" formality because the call was answered with their full name.
11Neighbor · Quick Question Direct
Best when you have confidence and a short ask
"Hey [Name]. My name is Jonathan — I have a quick question for you. You got 30 seconds?"
Use WhenCalling neighbors in a target zip. Doesn't lead with "Realtor" — just asks for the 30 seconds.
12Neighbor · Property-Specific Bait
Pure interrupt — they have to correct you
"Hi, I'm calling about the property on [Address]. You don't still own that, do you?"
Use WhenYou want them to engage immediately. They almost always correct the assumption — and now they're talking.
13Neighbor · Buyer Hook
"Completely opposed" is genius framing
"There's been a lot of buyers looking to purchase a home just like yours — if you had an offer that makes sense, is that something you'd be completely opposed to?"
Use WhenCold neighbor outreach. Easier to say "no, not completely opposed" than "yes, I might sell."
14Neighbor · Referral Open
Sidestep the sell — make them feel useful
"Hey [Name] — Jonathan with Keller Williams. Quick question, then I'll get out of your hair: do you happen to know anyone in your neighborhood who's been talking about moving? I'm working with buyers who specifically want to be in your area."
Use WhenYou want to skip "are you selling" entirely. Often surfaces that THEY are the one thinking about it.
15Neighbor · Honest Reason
Specific local context lands harder
"Hi [Name]? Jonathan Diaso calling — I'm a Realtor in your area and I noticed something about your property I wanted to mention. But before I jump in: is now a bad time? I'd rather call you back than waste your two minutes."
Use WhenYou actually noticed something — long hold, recent comp, off-market potential. Specific hooks convert better.

Have market data ready. Know the DOM and list-to-sale ratio for their zip.

5
"And if you were to make a move, what are you looking for that you don't already have?"

Timeline
"If you decided to sell and got a great offer right away, you wouldn't be able to move anytime soon, right?"
Step 7
Meet
"Ok [Name], before I let you go — can I make a quick recommendation?"
Step 7
Cont.
"Okay [Name], one quick recommendation before I let you go. I know you're not moving until X — but imagine we sat down for thirty minutes, and by the end of it you walked away with a real plan for when that day actually comes. What it'll be worth, what to fix versus skip, what the tax picture looks like, where you'd go next. Is there any reason you wouldn't want that conversation now, while there's zero pressure?"
Pause. Right when they start to speak, use the Because framework.
Step 8
Because
"Because at the end of the day, you're a smart person — you're not going to make a move unless it actually makes sense for your family. All I want to do is help you see what 'makes sense' actually looks like, so when the time comes, you're making the call from a position of clarity instead of guessing."
Step 9
Set Apt.
"Ok, fine. Then what day are you thinking would make sense for us to get together?"
Step 10
Email
"Ok, I could probably make that work. In the meantime, would it be helpful if I emailed you a copy of my resume — or would you just end up deleting it?"
Pain Probes · Drop In When You Sense Tension

If they're stalling or going quiet, drop one of these — surface what's not working without pushing.

  • "What would have to change for it to even be worth thinking about?"
  • "What are you looking for that you don't already have?"
  • "When you imagine yourself five years from now, is this house part of the picture?"
  • "What's the one thing about this home you wish was different?"
Closing Questions · If They're Stuck

When the conversation stalls, these unlock the door.

  • "When's the last time someone helped you turn your goals into an actual plan?"
  • "If you did decide to make a move someday, do you have a close real estate agent you work with — or would you be open to giving me the opportunity to earn your business over time?"
  • "What are you looking for in a home that you don't already have?"
  • "If I sent you an email with valuable info on what's actually happening in your neighborhood, would you read it — or would you just end up deleting it?"
When They're Not Ready to Meet
"Would it be ok if I stayed in touch with you? Cool — what's a good email for you?"

"At some point you will want to buy or sell something — I'd love the opportunity to stay in touch."

"If the time ever came where you looked to buy or sell a home, do you have a close real estate agent you work with — or would you be open to giving me the opportunity to earn your business over time?"
Delivery Tips — General Prospecting
  • Smile while you dial. They can hear it through the phone.
  • Talk slower than feels natural. Slowing down signals confidence.
  • After Steps 4-5 (Discovery), shut up. Let them fill the space.
  • Never argue. If they push back hard, agree first, then ask one more question.
  • The goal of every call is the appointment, not the listing. Set the meeting even if they're soft on selling.

Two-step: confirm ownership, then earn the minute

2Honest Disarmer
Acknowledge what they're already thinking

Use WhenYou sense they're already defensive. Naming the call type first removes the pretense.
3Best Combo · Soft Confirmation
Two-step with permission
"Hey [Name] — do you still own the place over on [Address]?"

If yes:
"Perfect — my name's Jonathan, I work with property owners in that area. I had a quick question for you, totally up to you if you want to keep talking — do you have just a minute?"
Use WhenYou want maximum permission-based feel. "Totally up to you" disarms reflexive defensiveness.
4Out-of-State · Distance Pain
Lead with the eyes-on-it question

Use WhenTheir address is more than 200 miles from the property. The "eyes on it" question forces them to admit it's been a while.

Respectful, formal, slow pace
"Mr./Mrs. [Last Name], my name is Jonathan with Keller Williams. I'm calling about the property on [Address] — I can see you've owned it a long time, and I really don't want to take much of your time. Would it be alright if I asked one quick question?"
Use WhenRecords show 20+ year ownership. Slower pace, more formal address. Don't push for a meeting on the first call.

Delivery Tips — Absentee Owner
  • Open with empathy for the distance — even before they mention it. "Managing from far away" is universal pain.
  • Bring up taxes only after they bring up cash flow concerns. Don't lead with tax strategy.
  • Always offer Zoom as the meeting format. They can't drive over — make it easy.
  • Listen for tenant complaints. If they mention a problem tenant, the call just got a lot easier.
  • If they mention multiple properties, switch to the Portfolio script immediately.

Delivery Tips — PRBO
  • Acknowledge their rental strategy first. Never start with "but have you considered selling."
  • If they lived in the property in the last five years, get excited internally — Section 121 applies. Save it for the meeting.
  • "Most owners haven't seen the math side-by-side" is your strongest line for these calls.
  • If they've been struggling to rent it, soften your tone. They feel bad enough already.
  • End every call with the analysis offer, even if they're firm on renting.

Asset or Liability?

Most landlords bought the property as an asset. Over time it can quietly become a liability. Same house — different role in their financial life.

When it was an Asset
Rent covered the mortgage with room left over
Property appreciation outpaced ownership costs
Tenant was reliable, paid on time, low turnover
You had energy and time to manage it
Cap rate when purchased was 6–8%
When it became a Liability
Rent barely covers mortgage + tax + insurance
Maintenance and vacancy eat the cash flow
Tenant turnover or problem tenants stress the calendar
The calls and surprises feel like real weight
Cap rate on today's value is 3–4%

Hands them control. The timeline they pick becomes a real commitment — not a stall.
The Graceful Exit · Time to Decide
Time to
Decide
"[Name], I want to be respectful of both our time. I'm going to leave the door open on my side — if you ever want to talk, my number's saved. But I won't keep following up unless I hear from you, because honestly, I think you've got enough on your plate. Sound okay?"
The dignified release. Doesn't burn the bridge. They often come back six months later because you didn't chase them.
Follow-Up Discipline
  • Every call ends with a defined next step — even if it's just "I'll text you in two weeks."
  • Log everything immediately after — motivation, timeline, objection, next step.
  • Quarterly cadence is the floor. Monthly is too much. Never weekly.
  • Value-add touches outperform check-in touches by 3x. Always bring something.
  • When they go silent, the graceful exit is the strongest move — not another voicemail.

These are not sequential — they're tools to pull from. Apply across every call type as needed.

The Acknowledge · Agree · Isolate · Reframe · Close Sequence

A five-beat structure for any objection. The good responses you already use follow this — making it explicit means you can apply it to objections you've never heard before.

1
Acknowledge
"I hear you" / "That makes sense" / "Fair point"
Never start a response by arguing. Acknowledge first.
2
Agree
Find the part that's actually true and validate it. "You're right that..." / "Most owners feel that way..."
3
Isolate
"Is that the only thing holding you back, or is there something else?"
Surfaces the real objection underneath the stated one.
4
Reframe
Turn the objection into a reason to meet, not a reason not to. "Actually, that's exactly why a twenty-minute conversation would be useful..."
5
Close
Direct ask for the next step. "Would Tuesday or Wednesday work better?" / "Fair?"
The "Three Numbers" Framework

Every property conversation reduces to three numbers. Master these and you control the conversation.

N°
1
What it's worth today. Current market value. Anchors every other number.
N°
2
What you'd actually walk away with. After taxes, fees, payoffs. This is the number that shocks people.
N°
3

The Pain · Possibility · Permission Sequence

Three-beat structure for any first call. Use it as the spine.

Pain
Surface what's not working. "How's it been managing the property? Is it pretty smooth, or has it gotten heavier than you signed up for?"
Possibility
Paint the alternative. "If you didn't have this property a year from now, what would you do with the time or money?"
Permission
Ask for the next step. "Would it be helpful to see what the math actually looks like? No pressure either way."
Listening Rules
During the Call · Things That Win the Conversation
  • After a now/after question — silence for 5 full seconds. Most agents kill the answer by talking too soon.
  • When they share something emotional — acknowledge it. "That sounds really hard" before "let me show you the options."
  • When they ramble — let them. The longer they talk, the more committed they become.
  • Take notes on their exact words. Use them back later in the call and in follow-ups.
  • If you don't know the answer to a question — say so. "Honest answer, I don't know — let me find out and call you back."
Asset vs Liability · The Mental Reframe

The single most important reframe in the whole playbook. Most owners bought their property as an asset. Quietly, over time, it can become a liability. Same house — completely different role in their financial life. The question is whether it's still doing what they originally bought it to do.

The Question Every Owner Should Be Asked

"If your house was a stock and you didn't already own it, would you actually buy it today at this price for the rent it brings in?"

When it's still an Asset
Rent covers mortgage with cushion left over
Property appreciation outpacing carrying costs
Stable tenant, low turnover, low stress
Owner has energy and time to manage
Cap rate is healthy on today's basis
When it's quietly become a Liability
Rent barely covers expenses, sometimes doesn't
Deferred maintenance growing every year
Tenant turnover, problem tenants, vacancy stress
The calls and surprises feel like real weight
Cap rate on today's value is 3-4%
The Strategic Advisor Mindset

A note for context, not for stating out loud.

A Long-Game Relationship
  • You're not closing a transaction today — you're earning the right to be the advisor over decades.
  • The best clients come from relationships built quietly over months and years, not from aggressive closes.
  • Honest advice — including telling them not to sell — is what builds the trust that leads to the eventual referral.
  • Your patience is your edge. Most agents bail at three months of quiet. The relationships that pay you back are the ones who hear from you in month nine and year three.

Exit with Tax Protection
01
1031 to Delaware Statutory Trust
Out of management. Still in real estate.
When it fits
Older owners ready to retire from management entirely.
How
Sell, 1031 into fractional ownership of institutional-grade real estate. Monthly checks, zero work.
Win
Out of management. Income continues. Taxes deferred. Heirs get step-up basis.
"Imagine getting a check every month from real estate you don't have to think about. That's a DST. Still in real estate — just done being a landlord."
02
1031 Exchange to Higher-Yield Market
Same equity. Double the cash flow.
When it fits
Cash-flow-focused owners frustrated by sub-4% cap rates.
How
1031 into Texas, Arizona, Tennessee, Florida — 6-8% cap.
Win
Higher cash flow plus zero tax hit at sale.
"Your property is making you maybe 3% net. Same equity in Phoenix is making 7. You don't pay tax on the trade."
03
1031 Trade-Up · Consolidate
Three problems become one asset.
When it fits
Small portfolio owners tired of managing multiple properties.
How
Sell 2-3 underperformers, 1031 into a single NNN commercial, larger MF, or medical office.
Win
One tenant or property manager. Cleaner taxes. Better return profile.
"You've got three properties and three sets of problems. Trade them for one tenant with a 10-year lease who pays you to do nothing."
04
Installment Sale · Seller Financing
Become the bank.
When it fits
High-basis owners worried about a big tax year.
How
Seller carries paper. Capital gains spread over years of payments. Interest income on top.
Win
Tax smoothing, monthly income, secured by the property.
"You become the bank. Buyer pays you principal and interest for 10-15 years. Your tax hit is spread out, not one ugly bill."
High-Impact Opportunities
05
Section 121 Exclusion
$250K to $500K of gains · tax free.
When it fits
Owners who lived in the property for 2 of the last 5 years.
How
Primary-residence exclusion wipes out up to $250K (single) or $500K (married) of capital gains.
Win
Most owners forget this exists. Free money for asking the question.
"Did you ever live in this property? Two of the last five years — the first $250K of gain is tax free. $500K if married. Most agents won't ask."
06
Highest & Best Use · ADU Strategy
Is your lot worth more than your building?
When it fits
Older property on a larger lot, especially in ADU-eligible or SB-9 zones.
How
Analyze whether redeveloping, adding ADUs, or selling to a developer beats the current use.
Win
Unlock value most owners don't see.
"What if your lot is worth more than your building? Your single-family might be a four-unit waiting to happen."
07
Off-Market Quiet Sale
No signs. No showings. No tenant disruption.
When it fits
Owners with tenants in place who don't want disruption.
How
Marketed only to investor network. No MLS, no open houses, often no inspections.
Win
Discreet exit. Tenant never knows until close. Closes in 14-30 days.
"You don't have to put a sign in the yard. I have investors who'd buy it tomorrow with the tenant in place — no showings, no disruption."
Keep & Optimize
08
Cash-Out Refinance
Access the equity. Keep the asset.
When it fits
Owners who like the property but feel equity is "stuck."
How
Refinance to 65-70% LTV, pull tax-free cash, redeploy elsewhere.
Win
Liquidity without triggering capital gains.
"You don't have to sell to access the money. A cash-out refi gives you the equity tax-free and the property keeps doing its job."
09
Cost Segregation
Keep the property. Wipe out paper income.
When it fits
High rental income, high tax bills, owner wants to keep the property.
How
CPA + cost-seg engineer break property into components, accelerate depreciation.
Win
More cash flow today without selling.
"You can keep the property and stop paying tax on the income. Cost segregation accelerates the depreciation."
10
Step-Up Basis · The Hold Strategy
Sometimes the right move is no move.
When it fits
Older owners (70+) with significant gains, no income pressure, heirs in line.
How
At death, heirs inherit at stepped-up basis. Capital gains wiped clean.
Win
Best answer for the right person. Your honesty builds long-term trust.
"Honest answer — if you don't need the money and your kids will inherit, selling now is the worst tax move. Hold it, they get it tax-free."
